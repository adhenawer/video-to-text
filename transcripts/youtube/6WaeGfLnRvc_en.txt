[2:37] EMPIRE BUILDING: WHY COUNTING HEADS RULES

Empire building is a bad thing because it focuses more on the raw number of people than on what you're doing or the impact you're having. Why does it exist? Because it's rewarded. [13:10] THE HIDDEN CHESS OF REORGS

The triggering condition for a reorg is usually a change in the business. Something has changed and the organization isn't set up to address it — a new market, an acquisition, a new project. The second reason is somebody else quit or got moved, so reorgs come due to cascades.

All managers know that too many reorgs cost you people, so you don't want to do them too often. What that means is you wait as long as you can, and then when you need to do one, you're asking: how do we get everything we want? If there's someone we don't like, how do we give them less so they'll move on? If there's a high performer, how do we set them up for success? How do we retain key people? Hopefully the start of the reorg is "how do we make the business better?" But as soon as we figure that out, we're trying to figure out how much else we can throw on the deck as the ship is pulling away.

There are usually multiple people involved. Sometimes you have to get them on board with your second motive: "Yeah, I want to help this person get promoted as a part of this reorg. Are you supportive of that?" Sometimes you have to tell a story like, "No, I'm not doing that, it's really this reason." You're a storyteller, coming up with your narrative about why this is the best possible reorg — rewarding the deserving people and accomplishing the business goals. If anybody does suffer, you've made sure it's people who aren't the highest performers.

When I think about reorgs I've done, they're very much about retention. People don't like change. Who is it I absolutely need to keep? And who is it that if they leave, I'll either be okay or even maybe happy? I can't think of a time where I put someone in a role thinking "by putting them there, they'll quit." But I can absolutely think of the reverse — people I really wanted to keep, using the reorg to give them something juicier.

There's a hidden chess going on. You're aware of multiple things, and aware of the messenger: this is what this person wants to hear, and here are the things I'm trying to achieve. What pieces can I sell to make the reorg happen? That turns off a lot of engineers. They say, "I don't want to manage all these things, I just want to say it as it is." But you can certainly work around it — you can do a reorg purely on business goals without any concern for people, but that's probably going to cost you people.

[1:05:05] INTERPERSONALLY WARM, PROFESSIONALLY FIRM

I call it being interpersonally warm: friendly, smiling, not getting agitated, while being professionally firm — saying what I need about what I really want, but not bringing anger or "you versus me" into it.

A great phrase is "help." "How can you help me? I want to get to the next level." Everyone loves to think of themselves as helpful. That's different from "What are you going to do for me?"

When I stop seeing my boss as "this person controls my pay, I have to yank it out of them," and instead as a human trying to get by, I can say, "Look, here's what I need. [1:20:30] BE A PORCUPINE: ORG VS ORG

A very smart engineering leader once said: "Respect between two engineering teams is inversely proportional to their distance." [1:36:52] UMBRELLA MANAGERS VS FUNNEL MANAGERS

[1:46:47] BACK CHANNELING

I think of back channeling in two ways. Manipulative back channeling is where I'm trying to do something I wouldn't be willing to say in public — undermining, painting the other leader negatively.

Positive back channeling is giving people a chance to ask questions and find out their concerns without bringing them out in public. Maybe I'm the CFO concerned about the budget but I don't want to put you on the spot. In a back channel, I can ask: "Here's my idea. Any concerns?" The negative version is the backroom deal — the kickback. "You support this and I'll make sure you're taken care of." I'm coming with a proposal that amounts to: you should do the wrong thing because we both benefit. Even though I'm a snake, I'm not going to bite you. We're snakes together.

[1:55:41] WHY PEOPLE REALLY GET FIRED

There are a lot of rules about when you can and can't fire someone, so almost always the public story is performance. That's the one thing that's allowed.

The hidden reasons are usually incompatibility. The main one I see is style differences between the person and their leader. One is detail-oriented, one is high level. With engineers, often one wants to be tech-oriented — what's the best technology — and the other is business-outcome-oriented — what makes the money, I don't care if it's a hack. It becomes a religious debate. Over time, small things become labels: "that person is technically clueless," "that person's only about money." Once it's personal, you have a lens. So many things are ambiguous, and you'll interpret everything they do as bad. That's where people get fired, but it's always labeled as performance.

One of the most blatant harassment cases I saw was an executive hired at chief level who immediately started harassing women. So blatant that when we asked one of the HR representatives on his team if she'd gotten complaints, she started crying because he had harassed her too. The gall to harass a woman in human resources. He was terminated right away.

How did he get hired? We didn't have legal proof. We had many people saying it happened, we believed them, but never took it to court. If I name him and say "don't ever hire Fred Smith, he harasses women," Fred can sue me — I have no legal proof. So when someone calls asking about Fred, I refer them to HR, and HR says "Fred was employed from this date to that date." That's how these people keep getting hired. I looked him up on LinkedIn — the fact he ever worked at Amazon isn't on his resume. He just deleted it. Moved on.

[2:05:57] FIRING MANAGERS IS HARDER

It is harder to fire managers. Three reasons. One: they're more experienced. They don't have obvious bad performance — the first person I let go stopped coming to work. Managers don't make mistakes at that level. Two: a manager's work is hard to measure. Did they hire? Did features ship on time? Bug count? It's a "he said, she said" problem. Three: managers are part of the system. They know what termination looks like — they get spidey sense and start positioning themselves to be hard to get rid of.

My ideal way is to surface the idea socially. "I don't think we've gotten off to the best start here, and I'm wondering how you feel about it. Would you like my help finding some other option?" Very nebulous. Smart managers get the message: I'm your boss. I'm not happy. I'm offering you a door — time to find a job, maybe a severance package. Do you want to do this the easy way?

They won't say "Of course, you're right, I'm failing." A smooth manager will say, "I'm so sorry you're not satisfied. Of course I'm doing my utmost" — meaning, I can make this hard for you, you're going to pay for my exit. "I want you to be happy with my work. If you're not, I'd like to discuss what changes we could make" — meaning, I'm open.

I never used the word "fired" or "performance," because that creates something legally called constructive termination. If I've said "we might have to fire you, do you want to quit first?" that's a legal problem — anything I do from that point is seen as me making up reasons. So I avoided it. They equally avoided acknowledging any problem. "I'm sorry you feel that way." That's your problem, you feel weird things. Both sides know exactly what's happening.

The other path is classic performance management — document goals, document they missed them. Almost all managers will quit before that completes. They know "this is going to take ninety days, I have ninety days to find a new job."

[2:12:31] YOUR LEVERAGE WHEN BEING FIRED

I'm a little more cynical. They're paying severance for something. What's the leverage?

First: this process takes a lot of manager time. You have to document, follow process, file paperwork. HR audits. The first offer I make: "I'll save you all that time if you give me money to look for another job."

Second: the story I tell the team. "I can leave talking about what a terrible experience this was and what a monster you are. Or I can leave saying this wasn't a fit for me, I'm excited to be moving to Acme Inc, and I can't speak highly enough about how Ryan treated me through this. Which story do you want me telling my co-workers?"

You deliver it as polite fiction. "Ryan, so far you've been nothing but supportive. If I were to move on someday, I would of course explain how supportive you've been. But if we're discussing my moving on, I would hope you'd want to support my transition financially so that I can tell that story."

Buy time. If they're giving you performance feedback, say: "Ryan, I appreciate you're giving me this feedback. I know we need to have this conversation. Honestly, this moment I can't have it productively. Can we have it tomorrow morning?" Most managers won't say no, because they're already on edge. If they say no — now they have a beef with HR. "He was so mean. I asked for space. He said no, I've got to ridicule you right now."

Your last piece of leverage: your manager doesn't want to feel bad about themselves. They want to feel good. If they can spend a little of the company's money — which isn't really their money — so they can look in the mirror and say, "It didn't work out with Ryan, but I took care of him," they want to do that. Most managers hate firing. They avoid it. One reason people don't survive firings is by the time the manager decides, they've told themselves they're out of options. Getting back off the brink is very unlikely.

People come to me: "Ethan, I've just been put on a performance improvement plan. Help me survive it." I tell them: "Honestly, no. You're most likely going to be fired." By the time they get around to a formal plan, they've tried everything else. They just want you gone as quietly as possible. People write back three months later: "You were right. I was fired."

A softer phrasing for you: "This is such a hard message, and I have so many friends on this team. I don't want them to think badly of me. I don't want them to think this is an unfair environment. Can you work with me so this is a graceful exit?" Later: "For it to feel good, I need enough security to get to my next thing. I know you want that. I know you don't want anyone to suffer."

[2:24:37] GETTING UNSTUCK AT STAFF ENGINEER

What gets people stuck is they're not learning the new skill of the next level. They're doing the current skill harder. What gets you promoted early is hard effective work — delivery, shipping, building. But as you get further, you need different skills: delegate, communicate, let go of your hard skills. If you micromanage — "write it this way, build it that way" — you'll drive away your good people.

There's a classic book by Marshall Goldsmith: "What Got You Here Won't Get You There." It shows a ladder with a gap. Talk to people a level or two above. Ask them: "What did you have to let go of? What did you have to learn? What do you see holding me back?"

Getting advice is better than getting feedback. If I ask you for feedback, I'm asking you to do something hard — criticize me. If I ask for advice, everyone has advice to share. They'll give you the same information more positively.

I see so many people working brutally hard but ineffectively.

[2:27:36] HOW TO WIN AGAINST AN EQUAL PEER

Assume you're both equally qualified. You're not going to win on skill. So: relationship and trust.

Trust is: do I have to look over my shoulder wondering if you're doing things that won't make me look bad? Will we both be successful? As a senior leader, I don't have time to micromanage. I need to give you something and assume it will get handled.

Trust is earned more in bad times than good. My favorite analogy is violent: two people in a war together. When the bullets were flying, you came through — I know you'll come through the rest of the time. At work, that's a crisis, a big outage, a lost account. If you jump on that and fix it, even if it costs you — you cancel a vacation (not that I recommend doing that a lot) — I will remember: when the chips were down, when we lost the Acme account, Ryan flew back from Hawaii early and made it. That's trust.

Second: do I like you? If I have two equally qualified people and I'm going to spend more time working with one, why would I not choose the person I enjoy? Some engineers hate this idea — if they were dead, they'd be spinning in their graves. Look, if I have to spend eight hours a day with someone, I'm going to be drawn to the person who's fun. It doesn't mean kiss up to everyone. It does mean: be friendly, be pleasant, be that helpful smiling person.

Bezos put it: when you come into the room, do you suck all the oxygen out of it? Or are people like, "Oh, Ryan's here — this just got better"? Simple rubric: do people want you in the room? If they kind of don't, you're in trouble. If they kind of do, you're doing great.

I know a lot of engineers get pissed off by this. I'm not here because you'll like me — I'm here to get you where you want to go. I'm not here to apologize for the way the world works. The way the world works sucks, but it does work that way.

[2:32:54] GETTING CHAMPIONS FOR PROMO PACKETS

For senior promotions, you need champions or allies — people at that level or higher who'll vouch for you.

My favorite way: your boss on your side. You've got the team where your manager wants to get you promoted. I would email my peers or skips: "Ryan's going to be up for promotion in six months. Because your group is adjacent to ours, I'll need to ask you for feedback. So you've got six months to watch Ryan." Number one, me asking doesn't feel self-serving — I'm talking about you. Number two, six months later when that VP wants to say "I only saw Ryan twice," I can say, "I put you on notice."

I got good at getting my people promoted, and that allowed me to attract more talent. A guy who didn't get promoted — I said, "Look, I'm good at this, I'll get it done in six months, but you've got to give me till the next cycle." He said later: "That sucked, but I decided I'd give you six months." And we got it done. My biggest star employee went from level-five engineer, to senior, to manager, to senior manager, to director in eight years. Three promotions. He caught up to me and went to found his own company.

If your manager isn't that on board: go yourself. "I'm up for promotion. I'm pretty sure my manager will ask you for feedback. What can we do over the next six months so you're in a natural position to give it?" You need to oversubscribe — if you need four champions, ask six or seven, because one will leave, one will reorg, one will say no.

VPs know this is how the system works. They need your VP's help with their own people. They're good at understanding "this is the grease that keeps the thing flowing." They can write mild approving verbiage pretty quickly — maybe it looks like they cut and pasted and replaced "John" with "Ryan," but they've written a million of these.

I've never seen an engineer get promoted over their manager's objection. I can't promote you if the manager doesn't agree — I'd just be creating a mess, telling the manager they're wrong. Engineers envision: "I'll do such incredible work that I'll be recognized even though my manager is a bozo." It's not going to happen. You either get your manager on board or get out from under them.

[2:44:02] AVOIDING POLITICS: LEVERAGE AND GROWTH

Can you avoid politics? First: choose your leader. Find someone who shares your disdain for politics — maybe with a tech background — and wants to reward work. Second: go work on the hardest area where expertise will matter. Stand out.

Andy Jassy, before he was CEO of all Amazon, was CEO of AWS. He said principal engineers are normally expected to advise, mentor, do architecture — but "these few guys working on AWS, we just need them solving the hardest problems. I absolve them of all the other stuff." For engineers who don't want to review architecture or give promo feedback, he was the perfect boss. Their work was so essential he couldn't spare them.

If you have a skill no one else has and that skill is valuable, you'll get exceptions. I remember Andy talking about a high-level employee. Some criticized him — "he's not working at an Amazon level." Andy said, "Yeah, I think you're right, but he's in a role where we need him. He knows we need him. We're going to have to live with it." Andy was one of the hardest managers I worked for, but in this case: "Good on him. He's got us. We're going to have to live with it."

You have to have leverage. Live up to Cal Newport's "So Good They Can't Ignore You."

Growth changes everything. It's not twenty-five people wanting one promotion, it's lots of opportunity. My extreme example is the post office — the only roles are "carry the mail" or "be the postmaster." There's one postmaster. Until that person leaves or dies, you don't get that job. In fifteen years at Amazon, the company grew from 14,000 to 1.4 million people. A hundredfold. My first team was six people. Nine years later I had 800. That growth opens doors that in a normal company growing 5% or 10% a year will never happen. Find high growth.

[2:48:23] THE ONE THING HE WISHES HE KNEW EARLY

What would I tell early me? That it's not just being right. Not just being technically astute. There's all this other stuff going on — wake up to it. I was blind or deprioritized it. I thought, "That's management stuff." Even as an individual contributor, understand: management stuff does control the world. I wish I had been more aware.

To get it down to a single sentence: take it seriously that your relationships are what actually control your progress.

As an engineer, you want to believe it's your technical expertise that controls your career. It's not only that. It's your people skills. IQ without EQ is a waste of IQ. Cal Newport wrote "So Good They Can't Ignore You" — but the irony is he's a guy with the social skills to teach at Princeton and write books. He has the human skills to present his technical accomplishments delicately and well.

Humanity doesn't change. Read any ancient text of philosophy or religion — humans have been mean to each other since the first ape clubbed the other ape. I'm sorry that it frustrates you, but you're going to have to do it.
