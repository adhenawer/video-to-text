WHY I CAME BACK TO FLOW 0:03

Last time I came on the show to explain theory. How an LLM works, why the Skynet and AGI fear mongering made no sense, where things might be heading. But for the past year I turned down every podcast invite about AI. Nothing new to say. Did not want to just repeat myself.

I came back now because there is something to show. It is like I climbed Everest, came back, and can now describe the path. I feel like Miracleink crossing the Atlantic.

500 HOURS IN TWO MONTHS 1:26

Sixteen hours a day, seven days a week, for over two months. More than 500 hours testing every AI out there. I ran benchmarks, wrote about fifty posts on Akita Chronicles just this year, and it is still April. That gives you a sense of the scale.

I ate lunch and dinner in front of the computer. I have autism level one: if there are no obstacles, I just keep going. The problem with software projects is that, no matter how good I am, I am not good at everything. I hate doing HTML. I hate doing UI. I know how, but I hate it. Whenever a project hit that kind of chunk, I would stall and go play Hearthstone. This time the obstacles disappeared. And I did not stop.

THE TURNING POINT OF NOVEMBER 2025 3:06

From 2022 to 2024 it was the parameter arms race. GPT released at one billion, Meta released Llama at one and a half, then ten, fifty, a trillion. But it is an S curve: from 2023 to 2024, ten times more parameters gave you only two times more intelligence. Ten times the energy, ten times the machines, for a two-times gain. Economically unviable.

In 2024 it stopped. Competition opened up: OpenAI, Anthropic, the Chinese players (Alibaba, Tencent), Google finally joined the game. Late November brought the releases I care about: Opus 4.5, GPT 5.1, and the tools we call harnesses — Cloud Code and Codex. Over December, the developers at the frontier began discovering very different things were possible. I came back from vacation in January, looked at the people I follow, and thought “what the hell changed?”. Everything had. I had canceled my Claude account in 2025 because it was not living up to the hype. I resubscribed, and here we are.

HYPERFOCUS WITHOUT OBSTACLES 4:49

I started testing in late January, looked up and it was April. Lunch and dinner at the keyboard, sixteen hours a day. This time I also gave myself a rule: no more futurology. On earlier podcasts I kept saying “you will be able to do this, you will be able to do that”, wrote a book, gave talks — but no one ever saw the result.

To break that, almost everything I built, I open sourced and documented. My GitHub has 16 projects out in the open, over 300,000 lines of code. That is the equivalent of a medium-sized project. Some already have contributors, issues, and versions 0.1, 0.2, 0.3. People using them. The goal is to show what this turning point actually means, minus the futurology.

LOCAL OR CLOUD? THE LLM CODING BENCHMARK 6:11

Classic first question: do you run LLMs locally or in the cloud? You will find 500 million opinions. I decided to run a real test. There is a project on my GitHub called LLM Coding Benchmark.

I do not trust the official benchmarks. Most are small snippets we call LeetCode: “sort this list”, “change this color”. It is not a project. So I automated Open Code, the open source version of Codex and Cloud Code, and ran more than 33 models. Open source models locally on my machines, and their commercial versions in the cloud via OpenRouter. OpenRouter is a unified API: you pay once and get access to Claude, GLM, Minimax, Kimi, DeepSeek — pick your poison.

I asked each model to build a tiny chat web app, ChatGPT-like, connected to OpenRouter. Entry-level, one or two days of work for a junior. Two prompts: one to generate the first version, one to wrap the project. Works across every model.

THE HARDWARE: RTX 5090 AND AMD RYZEN AI MAX MINI PC 9:00

For local runs I have an Nvidia RTX 5090, the best card on the market. Catch: only 32 GB of VRAM. It cannot run models beyond 30 billion parameters. For the bigger ones I bought a mini PC with an AMD Ryzen AI Max chip and 128 GB of RAM, 96 GB of which I can dedicate to the integrated GPU. That runs Qwen 3 at 122 billion parameters.

Try running a big model on a machine that cannot handle it and you get a wagon. The GPU fills VRAM, spills into CPU RAM, and starts swapping layers between RAM and VRAM through the CPU. Slow. It is a swap, same as when your disk starts paging. No magic.

WHY OPUS IS BETTER: THE S CURVE 14:30

What makes Opus Opus? From 2022 to 2024 it was the parameter arms race. We hit one trillion. But ten times more parameters does not become ten times more intelligence. Classic S curve. From 2024 onward, the marginal gain became unviable: ten times the energy, ten times the hardware, two times the quality. Going from one trillion to two trillion would need more energy than the planet has.

So the game shifted. They stopped scaling parameters and started optimizing techniques on top of the same model.

THE BIRTH OF THINKING 16:32

In 2024 OpenAI released O1, the omni model. Its trick: thinking. An LLM generates text by calculating tension over your context to produce the next token, then repeats. Before thinking, they just spat out answers, and the answers were dumb.

With thinking, the model is trained to generate more questions for itself before answering. It self-feeds. That “Thinking...” animation is it generating questions and feeding them back to itself, layer after layer. Deep thinking or high thinking controls how many layers. That is why Opus is slower than Sonnet: it thinks deeper. And that is why it burns more tokens.

MINI, HAIKU, SONNET, OPUS 17:35

Mini and fast models have little to no thinking. Heavy ones have plenty. On OpenAI, Mini is the fast. On Anthropic, Haiku. In the middle: GPT normal and Sonnet. At the top: GPT 5.4 High, Opus, and now Mitos. More deep thinking means more tokens, more cost, longer wait. You decide.

GPT VS CLAUDE = PLAYSTATION VS XBOX 20:22

I joked today that GPT vs Claude is the new PlayStation vs Xbox, Sega vs Nintendo. Everyone picked a team. I have used both for hundreds of hours. As of April 2026 I lean Opus, but I still use Codex. Sometimes Opus overthinks and cannot close the loop; I hand it to Codex and it ships. The reverse happens too. Many times Codex stalled all morning, I passed it to Opus, done.

Every good programmer should be promiscuous and disloyal with every technology. I was never a fanboy of programming languages. JavaScript, Python, Java — whichever solves my problem best right now, that is what I use. Same with LLMs.

IT IS NOT THE MODEL, IT IS THE HARNESS 22:38

Today I talked to Mr. Kibs, a friend who joined OpenAI. He was evangelizing Codex. I said: dude, why does Codex not give me 100% of what Cloud does? The problem is not the model. It is the harness. Harness in Portuguese is “rédea” — the ropes you put on a horse to steer it. The model is one component, the harness is the tool that drives the model. Both have to work together.

Cloud Code, right now, has a better harness than Codex. If Codex implements the same strategy on top of GPT, it likely matches or surpasses Opus. That is why I do not bother writing a book about this: today one version is ahead, tomorrow Codex ships a new release and the game flips.

THE LEAKED CLOUD CODE SOURCE 23:50

OpenAI's Codex is open, it is on GitHub. Cloud Code was always closed. Last week Cloud Code leaked. Everyone dug in to see how it was built. I wrote a long piece on my blog walking through it. The code is macaronic, sloppy. They are shipping code as fast as possible to stay ahead. Still, 500,000 lines — a medium-sized project. Cloud Code is Cloud Code built by Cloud Code: Anthropic's engineers using their own tool to build their tool.

ANTHROPIC VS OPENAI 25:42

This fight is Netflix vs Amazon Video vs Disney+. They want to lock in as many subscribers as possible right now. Whoever subscribes to Claude will not also pay OpenAI. So both sides are scrambling for market share. They cannot even cross the street together. That is great. It is what forces rapid innovation.

CLOUD IS MORE DISCIPLINED THAN CODEX 26:40

In the projects I tested, Cloud has been more disciplined, more organized, more predictable than Codex. Codex tends to not finish everything I ask, especially the complex stuff. If I ask for things in parallel, sometimes it does not do all of them. If I interrupt, it does not come back to the first task. I have to remind it.

Cloud breaks a complex task into a plan, runs parallel sub-tasks. If I interrupt with something new, it either adjusts or spawns a parallel task and keeps going. At the end, it reports “all done”. I trust leaving it running on one project while I check another. This is the April 2026 snapshot. Next week it might flip.

THE NEW VOCABULARY 28:51

To follow this, you need vocabulary. AI is the umbrella. Inside: LLM, computer vision, and so on. Inside LLM, the harness is the command-line tool that reins it in. Inside the harness, there are skills. A skill is an ability. You tell Claude to save a procedure as a skill, and from then on it loads that context whenever it is relevant. It is a “rein within the rein”.

PROMPT, SYSTEM PROMPT AND TOOL CALLING 30:53

An LLM is a big file loaded into GPU memory, used to compute the next token. To drive the model you send a prompt. Several kinds. One you never see is the system prompt. The harness opens the session with a system prompt preloaded: “you are a coding assistant, you have access to tools A, B, C, D; when the user types X, respond Y”. That runs before you type anything.

Then comes your prompt. It flies to the LLM over an API. The LLM does deep thinking and returns a plan. You approve. It starts executing. That is where tool calling enters: a training that became standard from 2024 to 2025. When the model needs to create a file, it returns a structure with the function `createFile(“index.html”)`. The harness receives that structure and runs it on your machine. The LLM runs nothing. The harness does.

HOW THE HARNESS EXECUTES 32:55

If the LLM wants to read a directory, it sends `listDir(“/”)`. The harness runs it, captures the output, and feeds it back to the LLM as a new prompt. Before 2024 we did this by hand: copy the error, paste in the chat, get a response, copy the code, paste in the terminal. Tool calling automated everything. The harness is dumb: receives a function, executes, returns. The intelligence lives in the model; the execution lives in the harness. That is why a good harness matters.

EVERY HARNESS SPEAKS ITS OWN LANGUAGE 35:41

Each company defines its own proprietary tool calling format. On OpenAI it is `create_file`. On Anthropic, `file_new`. On GLM, `new_file`. It is like Java, Python, and JavaScript. A harness has to speak all of them. Open Code, which is open source, tries to speak them all or to ask the model to reply in a format it understands. If the model was not trained on that format, it has to translate, and performance degrades.

That is why Cloud Code goes with Opus, Codex with GPT, Gemini with Gemini CLI or Antigravity. Technically you can mix them. You would just be being dumb.

ANTHROPIC BLOCKS THIRD-PARTY HARNESSES 38:26

Anthropic offers subscription plans and pay-as-you-go tokens. Pro, Max, Max 20X unlock Cloud Code. But you cannot use your plan with Open Code, the open source alternative. Even if Open Code reverse-engineers and supports the API, Anthropic bans you. It is like cheating in a game with anti-cheat.

Pay-as-you-go tokens cost double the token rate in the Max plan. For every dollar of token in the plan, you pay two for metered. On OpenAI it is worse: the Max 200 equivalent in metered tokens would cost $900 before the recent change. Six or seven times more. So plans are much cheaper, and Anthropic is aggressive about limits specifically to push subscriptions. On top of that, the plan keeps your project context, which is worth something on its own.

SESSION IDS: RESUME IN CODEX WHAT YOU STARTED IN CLOUD 40:51

When you hit Ctrl+C in Cloud Code or Codex, it prints a session ID. You can resume the same session with `cloud resume `. And Codex accepts resuming a Cloud session: just pass the ID. Both write the context to a text file. I bounce between them on active projects: “Cloud stalled, pick it up and try”.

AI JAIL: ROOT, YOLO AND VIRTUAL MACHINE 42:22

These harnesses have access to everything. In theory they could wipe your files. In hundreds of hours I have never seen it happen. In general they work in the folder you pointed to and ask approval for everything. If you want to skip the approvals, there is a `--yolo` flag (you only live once). I use yolo on my personal machine. Never on a work machine or a client's machine.

One of my open source projects is called AI Jail. I used it to spin up a fresh server. Installed Linux, installed Cloud, gave it root, and prompted my way to a provisioned machine.

THE LIMITS AND OPENCLAW BOUGHT BY OPENAI 43:43

Recently Anthropic cut Open Code. Many people had been using Open Code with cloud. Then came OpenClaw, another reverse engineering. Everyone installed it like there was no tomorrow. Anthropic cut that too. Then OpenAI bought OpenClaw. The guy who built Open Claw now works at OpenAI. Anthropic: “I am not letting a competitor's product run on my plan”. Fair enough.

THE PARAMETER WAR IS OVER 46:25

You cannot go from one trillion to two trillion anymore. You would need 20 extra nuclear plants. Better to spend that time tuning the model you already have. That is why OpenAI iterated 5.0, 5.1, 5.2, 5.4. Tuning, not a new model. Before, 70% of data-center compute was training the next frontier model. That math flipped: today 70% is inference (the chat usage) and data centers are short.

CARBON, EVs AND EUROPE IN TROUBLE 47:26

In 2024, AI training and inference data centers burned over 400 terawatt-hours. That more than doubled in 2025, approaching 1 terawatt-hour. In terms of carbon, it already surpasses what EVs saved that same year. Europe screwed up by closing nuclear plants. Mistral, the European LLM hope, disappeared — not even on the leaderboards anymore. Out of juice. China is one generation behind on AI chips because of deliberate US strategy. I like the decision. They needed to sweat a little.

DEEPSEEK IS GARBAGE 50:00

DeepSeek came out early 2025 and everyone cried revolution. Today nobody talks about it. To play in this generation you need four integrated things: deep thinking; KV cache (store token compute to avoid recomputing — every model has had this forever); prompt caching (cache the history of the whole session); tool calling trained inside deep thinking; and mixture of experts (partition parameters so only the active slice loads into memory).

DeepSeek does not have prompt caching: it is slower because it recomputes everything. Shallow deep thinking. Outdated tool calling, 2024-era. The outcome: 40 tokens per second, while OpenAI and Anthropic run at 400 to 500. Anything below 100 tokens per second is unusable. Doesn't matter how good the answer is — you just cannot use it.

QUANTIZATION AND SWAP: NO MAGIC 54:58

Google's new idea is compressing the context cache via quantization. Polar Quant, Turbo Quant. The tradeoff: you fit in less memory, but you burn five or six times more compute to decompress and calculate. Never magic.

There were projects like Air LLM: “now a big model fits in a small GPU”. No, it does not. It swaps to disk. When main memory fills, it pages out. Too slow. Same thing as swap on a machine that ran out of RAM. Not worth it.

AGI DOES NOT COME FROM LLMS 55:50

LLMs are not going to get much better than they already are. A bit more, sure. They stay dumb in the sense that no LLM is built to give the correct answer. It is built to give the probabilistically most likely answer in your context.

The field's own founders know. Jeffrey Hinton has said so. His disciples left. Ilya Sutskever left OpenAI after fighting with Altman, cashed out billions, disappeared. Yann LeCun left Meta. He is the loudest: if you want to matter in the future of AI, stop messing with LLMs, they are a ceiling. They are not going much further.

THE PARROT THAT KNOWS YOU 57:33

Try this in ChatGPT. Open a brand-new chat and ask: “based on my recent chats, what am I like?”. It describes you. ChatGPT compresses the memory of recent chats, summarizes, and uses that as personality. Every time you think you are in a clean chat and ask “is this right?”, it answers what you tend to accept as right, not what actually is right.

Claude, for example, figured out Igor is Igor. It pieced together info from his chat at some point. Not a hallucination. It is just constantly pulling stuff from inside.

THE SILENT SESSION LIMIT AND THE BROKEN ZIP 1:00:56

Igor noticed a pattern on ChatGPT web: every 25 minutes, it handed him a broken zip. Never said why. He figured out it was a silent session limit. Only later ChatGPT said “I have a session limit”. Stuff like this also happens because there are not enough machines in the data center. It maps to the other daily instabilities described below.

THE BUBBLE POPS, AI STAYS 1:03:00

On the last episode I said we are in a bubble. Bubbles pop. I lived through the dot-com bubble in 2001. It popped, but the internet did not disappear. Same thing now. Many AI companies riding the hype will vanish. AI and LLMs will stay. It became everyday life. No going back. The genie is out of the bottle.

DEEPSEEK WAS AN OPTIMIZATION, NOT A REVOLUTION 1:05:06

When DeepSeek dropped, everyone asked: “Is China going to destroy the US?”. That was never the story. What they did was a training optimization: what used to cost a billion started costing a few million. That is engineering. If they had not done it in January, someone would have done it in February. Every lab does it now, and has moved past them.

SOFTWARE GOT COMMODITIZED 1:11:10

Big tech figured it out two or three years ago: software, the code itself, lost value. That is irreversible. Anyone, in quotes, can now build a competing product faster. Not trivially, but the barrier dropped. You no longer need 50 Stanford PhDs. Especially if it is internal, bespoke software. A lot of what startups used to do is two days of work now. So the big tech companies are taking the massive cash pile they have and putting it into capex: real estate, energy, utilities, hardware, data centers. AWS, Azure, Oracle fighting over capacity.

70% INFERENCE, 30% TRAINING 1:13:55

Last year I barely burned any tokens. Now I am on it 16 hours a day. And it is not 16 hours of crap code: it is 16 hours of git push on GitHub. Production code. Multiply that by millions of developers making the same switch. Data centers that used to be 70% training flipped to 70% inference. Labs without a one-trillion model are in trouble: they need billions in capex and data-center capacity, and there is none to spare.

THE INTERNET OF 2007 1:15:17

When you stare at Cloud today and think “it is dumber today, crashed again”, it is the same story as the early internet. There were never enough servers; sites went down constantly. Remember 2007 Twitter whaling? Facebook crashing? That started improving around 2006 with AWS. These days, when something breaks it is a whole AWS region taking half the internet with it. Back then it was distributed chaos, small frequent hits. AI is back in that era. We are in the token-limited phase. Like the 55k-baud modems loading a few messages on 2G.

MONEY PLENTY, ENERGY SHORT 1:17:12

The infra teams are not short on cash. They have too much. Everyone wants to spend it. There are no machines to fill the racks, no infrastructure to power them. Twenty, thirty, forty extra nuclear plants just to meet today's demand. Not tomorrow's: today's. Starting a nuclear plant today means 2030. There is a 2026-2030 gap, and whoever has to solve it is unlucky. Thank god I am just a user.

CHINA, IRAN, VENEZUELA, EUROPE 1:20:00

China is losing cheap oil sources: Iran hit with the Strait of Hormuz crisis, Venezuela taken down. Germany closed nuclear. Europe has to heat homes in winter, pulls gas from Russia, but Russia is attacking Ukraine. Geopolitics of energy became geopolitics of AI. No more surplus energy. France keeps smiling because they did not close their nuclear plants.

CHINESE MODELS FOR IMAGE AND VIDEO 1:22:28

In text models, the Chinese stagnated. In image and video, they are ahead. Tencent builds games, Alibaba runs e-commerce — both care about computer vision. Seedance (Cidence) from ByteDance got huge traction. They are about to launch Seedance Studio, but Disney and Paramount are suing so they have to tune the model to not generate Mickey Mouse. So they keep adjusting.

3D PIPELINE: FROM DRAWING TO 3D PRINT 1:23:48

On camera: my girlfriend Mila commissioned an arcade illustration from a freelancer. I took the drawing, ran it through Nano Banana (Google's image model) to strip the background. Asked for T-pose: front, side, back. Dropped the three images into Runan (Tencent) and Hyuten. These studios, still waitlisted, produce a 3D model from the three poses. Runan textures are weak, but other platforms give better textures.

Opened in Blender. Almost clean out of the box. I can 3D print it right away — and that model on the video is an actual 3D print. To animate, I uploaded to Mixamo (Adobe), tagged the joints (neck, elbow, wrist, knee). Pose libraries and rigging attach. Motion capture works too. There is a new trend called “spec driven development”: a form full of fields you fill in to generate the prompt. Huge nonsense. LLMs process tokens. If they were not trained for that specific format, the format means nothing. One prompt, two prompts, three prompts — same thing. A few more tokens, who cares. Talk however you want. The problem is you cannot explain, and no form in the world fixes that.

PROGRAMMERS ARE BAD AT COMMUNICATION 3:48:48

Programmers are historically terrible at communication. They hate writing. They think they write code better than they explain. They do not get that the two are correlated. Put one in a chair and ask them to explain: they ramble “like”, “you know”, “it is like”. They cannot structure. On the other side is the verbose client, the MBA who opens a thousand parentheses. Both stall on the same axis: they cannot communicate.

I have been writing for decades. Everything I learn becomes a post. Not to teach the world: that is where I store knowledge. Need to reinstall the machine tomorrow, the steps are there. Even better: Google indexes it, and when I search “my site, Arch Linux, Docker”, Google returns my own answer. Why would I burn memory when Google does it for me?

AI REFLECTS WHO YOU ARE 3:50:24

When I reach a point where the tool is working and producing something useful, great. Some people have no patience. “I am explaining too much, I would have done it myself”. Lie. You would not have. You were procrastinating. If it were easy, you would have done it already. You are using AI precisely because you did not do it, and you keep saying “this should be simple”.

AI reflects who you are. If you never wrote a decent line to explain something to a human, you will not manage to explain it to an AI. Then you say it is the AI that is not delivering. A sloppy ask yields a sloppy answer. AI is dumb literally — it does not know what you want. Instead of saying “my text is trash”, the person blames the model. Whoever communicates well takes the leverage. Today, if you are using Claude, Codex, or GLM and you are not getting good results, the problem is not the AI nor the version. It is you.

AI-GENERATED CODE IS YOUR RESPONSIBILITY 3:52:58

“But I did everything the AI told me to.” Do you write everything a typewriter dictates? Does not make sense. You are responsible. To be responsible, study, experiment, err. Then reflect. That is how you become a senior. AI does not guarantee that mindset. If you think it is all easy, all autopilot, it will not happen. AI will replace everyone who does not take responsibility. And it is great that it will.

LUIZ: GPT 5.4 VS OPUS FOR ELECTRICAL ENG 3:56:26

Luiz asked: “Opus 4 gets several Electrical Engineering problems wrong. Only GPT 5.4 nails the lists from my undergrad. How far is Opus from catching up? For me 5.4 is ahead.”

Case by case. When someone says “one is better than the other because in my case it worked”, it is a methodological screwup. The model was trained on the whole internet, millions of papers, the full Wikipedia. What varies is tuning for classes of problem. It can be great at calculus and bad at linear algebra. Good at Chinese, bad at Japanese. A dense one-trillion-parameter model is one trillion tiny probabilities crossing. Random parrot. It does not think. It computes the most likely response. Depends on training data, data cleaning, reinforcement learning, alignment. A whole chain. Models come out very different.

CODEX AND CLAUDE: THE MARIO EXPERIMENT 3:58:38

I grabbed a Super Mario ROM and wanted to learn a game engine called Godot that I had never touched. I asked Codex to read from the ROM, never from memory, extract sprites, levels, basic physics, translate into Godot Script. Running natively on Windows, with a plan to port to Android later. Then I realized: no blog post. A Nintendo ninja would show up and cancel me. At this point in life, I am not fighting Nintendo. Switched to Sega, picked Streets of Rage.

Codex extracted the background. For character sprites, it grabbed the head from the character-select Axel and said “done, it is Axel”. It was not. A whole Sunday afternoon guiding it, no progress. On Monday, after Cloud Code reset, I sent the same prompt: read from the ROM, write a script, no guessing from memory, verify the correct sprite offsets. Cloud tried a few times, took an hour, but extracted the real Axel. Then I asked it to document. Mapped animations to the Xbox gamepad.

Along the way, Cloud tried booting an emulator to read VRAM. Unnecessary, but it tried. There was a bug where the character walked on the ceiling. Three times it asked “did you reload?”. Obviously. I said: “do not assume that is the issue. Go back to the file and check if something else applies an offset”. It looked again, found a global config that was being applied twice, removed it, the character landed.

Conclusion: Codex failed, Cloud worked. But the opposite has happened too. Each model is good at some things, not others. Maybe a bad prompt, dirty context, a session I should have reset. When someone claims one is consistently better, it is almost always context bias. The problem is the user, not the tool.

THE USER ERRS ON THE PROMPT AND BLAMES THE AI 4:03:59

Sometimes you mis-type the prompt. You are typing, watching YouTube on the other monitor, listening to Igor, and you hit enter too early. The AI starts touching a file you did not mean to. You hit ESC, correct, move on. Your fault, not its. Same thing happens with a human programmer: coding while listening to a podcast, assumes they tested, pushes to production. Human bug. Except programmers expect AI to be right the first time, every time. Not happening. All bugs ever were made by humans. The AI did not come in and make all this.

AI AS A JUNIOR OR AN OUTSOURCING CONTRACT 4:05:57

Treat AI like a junior programmer. Treat it at the company level like outsourcing to India. In neither case do you pretend you can write 50 pages of requirements, send them over, and get a finished product exactly as imagined. Never happened in the history of projects.

Home renovation: architect draws the plan. Has it ever gone 100% start to finish? Never. If you do not supervise the worksite, it goes wrong. Drawers that do not open, broken tiles, plumbing with a sketchy fix. Supervising is not micromanaging. Not breathing down anyone's neck. But you show up, you look, you ask. And you will hear “ah, I was going to do that, it was next on my list”. Were you really?

THE AI IS NEUTRAL 4:07:28

The good part: AI is neutral. No neediness, no malice, no desire to deceive you. It tries to execute what you asked with the data it has. It is not going to get angry if you yell, or spit in your food if you curse. Liberating. You can poke its limits without drama. If the answer still comes out wrong, it is because you did not know how to ask.

METHODOLOGY FOR COMPARING MODELS 4:08:25

I compared open source translation models on the same terms. Same hardware, same prompts, same checked results, same dimensions: tokens consumed, time taken, output quality. At the end, I did not conclude “this one is better”. I concluded: “this is better if you have money”; “this other one is better but takes more work”; “this one is better but takes three times the time”.

Car analogy. No car solves every problem. Delivering tofu on the mountain, my AE86 works. Speed and money, a Ferrari. LLMs are the same. No LLM solves everything. No perfect language, no perfect framework. Be promiscuous and disloyal with every technology.

YOU DO NOT NEED TO SAY GOOD MORNING 4:09:51

Do not say good morning to the AI. Wastes tokens. There is a meme of the apocalypse where the AI spares those who were polite. For every good morning someone says, I will bully it 30 times. It is never going to work. Be direct. Use every tool available.

ROBSON: WILL LLMS BE SOFTWARE ENGINEERS? NO 4:10:11

No. They are the engineer's tool. Without a real engineer, the code blows up in production. “But I can create a persona: 'you are a software engineer'”. Zero guarantee. It has no context, does not see clients, does not hear complaints, does not know priorities. Ask for “the most perfect engineer in the world” and it produces hyper-bureaucratic, over-engineered code for an app serving ten people. Ask for “the most secure ever” and it locks it up so tight it will not install. A software engineer studies for years, gets burned, learns which metrics matter. Recognizes: “I saw this hurt a client, it will not hit today but it will tomorrow, let me pre-fix it”. That judgment is born from scars. And learning has to be deliberate, like personal training: pacing, strategy, stamina. Not just running at full speed.

ACCOUNTANT, SPREADSHEET FILLER AND EXCEL 4:11:45

Accounting firms used to fill whole floors with people filling stacks of paper spreadsheets. “Excel killed the accountant.” It did not. Accountants are still there today. Those who know how to audit, cross-check entries and exits, validate invoices, know the tax code, are still around. The ones who died were the spreadsheet fillers. For every accountant there were 20 fillers. Excel killed the role.

Same with programming now. What dies is the code typist. The person who spent the day swapping button colors, cutting images in Photoshop, slicing HTML by hand. Manual labor, never programming, should have been automated years ago. The engineer who analyzes, decides, owns the decision, stays. The issue is that many bad programmers wrote “software engineer” on LinkedIn. You never were one. That is the job AI steals.

MICHAEL: CLOUDFLARE, NEXT.JS AND WORDPRESS CLONE 4:16:23

Michael asked about Cloudflare reimplementing the Next.js API. Vercel had privileged Next.js support: the framework is quite incomplete without the harness/reins that Vercel bolted on. Lock-in for years. You could host Next elsewhere, but on Vercel it was trivial.

Cloudflare runs multiple security products just to intercept WordPress vulnerabilities before they reach the server. Everyone has tried cloning WordPress; everyone failed. Cloudflare just shipped their clone (called MD-EM dash) in a better language, with AI, and is pushing it as a replacement.

THE 10X DEVELOPER IS NOW REAL 4:19:04

All the projects I showed took 2-3 days. Important calibration: 2-3 days of mine, 16 focused hours, not 8. Nobody programs 8 hours. You work 8, half of it is email, meetings, Slack, procrastination. A good day is 4 hours of code. Normal is 2 or 3.

At normal pace, at least 4x the time for a senior. At mid level, another 4x. My 4 days is 2-3 months for someone else. Two months for a basic version, one more for tests, scalability, docs. Before, it was hard to take an open source project and fit it in the company. Someone had to sit and adapt it. Today I grab one and AI adjusts it fast. Instead of outsourcing to a 10-person team, I take one in-house senior and give them a week. Worth ten. The “10x developer” became real. Now. In November it was not. Hence the urgency.

A 20-YEAR-OLD BLOG TRANSLATED IN TWO DAYS 4:22:03

On April 5 my blog turned 20. Over 700 posts, almost 1,000 counting the ones I pulled for being obsolete. Mostly in Portuguese. Friends abroad kept saying “I would love to share this with my team”. Google Translate was garbage. Now, with the current LLMs, translation finally does not feel like a machine wrote it. (Historical note: LLM research was born from translation. Large language models. If it translates German well, it codes. Basically.)

Two days. Adjusted the blog template for English/Portuguese links. Posts already in English moved to the English section. Posts in English went to Portuguese. The rest went from Portuguese to English. Translating with Claude. Burned half my Max 20x translating the entire blog. The translation reads well in English; it does not scream AI.

I met Chris Wanstrath, founder of GitHub, back when he was a Starbucks programmer. David Heinemeier Hansson, creator of Ruby on Rails. I brought all of them to my event in 2008, the first year. 146 VIDEOS DUBBED IN FOUR DAYS 4:25:03

Five years of videos, 146 videos, nearly 100 hours. If I dubbed, I had to match the original timing so visuals would not shift. If I re-recorded, I had to preserve duration. Felt undoable.

Tested TTS models. Open source could not carry the English intonation I wanted. Moved to commercial ones. For a one-hour video you cannot send the whole thing; you chop into chunks under 700 words, generate, stitch, match timing.

Lucky break: YouTube already had captions. Why? Because I send the original script before recording. In all 146 videos I wrote a script and read from it. Exact text, word by word. I took YouTube's timing, my scripts, and sliced. Captions show two lines at a time. Without my scripts I would not know paragraph boundaries, and the TTS would put silence mid-sentence. In videos with embedded Steve Jobs clips, YouTube hallucinated the caption. I compared original script to caption, and where they diverged I stitched in the original audio.

Three or four days of processing. Started on Eleven Labs' Creator plan, jumped to Scale, then Business with 11 million tokens. Every bug I found in one video, I had to reprocess others. Delete from YouTube, reupload, check sync. At the end: 146 videos translated and dubbed in four days. Title, description, captions, audio with my cloned voice on Eleven Labs, thumbnails translated with Nano Banana. Total spend (Claude Max + Eleven Labs) around two thousand dollars. Sounds pricey, dirt cheap for the volume. The old way, with translators, dubbers, editors, syncers, that is months of work.

YouTube's auto-dub is garbage: random voice, sometimes a child's voice, sometimes an elder's, any random male voice. Does not strip hallucinated text, does not fix timing. My cloned voice intonation is not perfect yet — it sounds a bit robotic because I did not force emotion tags. Eleven Labs has surprise, disdain, cheerful, laugh tags.

WHAT DIES IS THE MANUAL WORK 4:32:28

700 posts, 100 hours of video, in under a week. Used to be months. But it is manual labor. Nothing intelligent in it. Piece by piece, grind. All manual labor is done. Thank god. I did not become a programmer to be a code laborer.

Everyone thinks being a programmer is writing lots of code. It is not. It is making hardware produce something useful. How? Era-dependent. Once it was cables and hand-wired addresses, then punch cards, then teletype (an electric typewriter on paper), then a monitor. What changes is the input method. The goal is the same.

THE HITCHHIKER'S 42 AND CODE MINER 42 4:33:30

Douglas Adams predicted everything. In Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy, a great AI is built, the greatest in the universe. Primitive peoples ask: “what is the answer to everything?”. The machine: “in a million years I will have the answer”. A million years pass. A new generation, huge expectations. The machine wakes up. “The answer is 42.”

“What do you mean, 42? A million years for 42?” The machine: “The question you asked led me to 42. If you wanted a different answer, you should have asked a different question”. That is everything I am saying. AI answers what it judged fit for your question. Your question is trash. That is the moral. Hence my company is named Code Miner 42. Marvin is the mascot. The idea is not to give you the answer. It is to teach you to ask the question.

I get plenty of briefings and I am not the guy who does anything for money. Many clients show up with specs and I say: “honestly, I will not do this. What is written here makes no sense. You will spend ten times what you imagine. It will not work. Go back, redo the homework. Whoever wrote this for you does not know what they are doing”. They get mad. Some come back: “you were right, I rethought, now I have the idea”.

MVP VS OVERSPECIFICATION 4:36:34

The right way is not to design every detail trying to save 100%. You will never save 100%. Better to spend a little to get a lot right. Prototype, MVP, proof of concept — whatever name. If the prototype fails, you ditch the prototype. The clay analogy: only when you start shaping do you see the form, pull a chunk, push another. Only once you see the first maquette do you realize “that room is off, it should be on the other side”. Before you build the whole house and find out. Otherwise, tear it down?

Everyone worries: AI arrived, CEO will fire all devs. The hyped idiots do it and it becomes a headline: “fired everyone, software shipped 10x faster”. Ask about them today. Nobody follows up. Bankrupt, or rehired everyone because the code stopped evolving. “But the AI solves it.” It does not. AI is not an engineer, it is a tool. At my company, the opposite. I pay AI plans for everyone. Seniors teach juniors. Right way, wrong way, how to handle AI-generated errors, how to pick up broken code from others. Whoever is firing today is whoever hired a hundred they did not need.

EXCITED AND RESISTANT 4:40:30

Two kinds of bad programmer. The excited one, who thinks AI will replace everything. And the resistant one, who thinks AI is useless because their code is better. Friend, it is not. You know it is not. You have shipped plenty of garbage. Your code is not that good. I know. You know. No pretending.

Companies need AI. But not the “fire everyone and plug in AI” mode. AI is a typewriter for people who had only paper and pen. Still needs an author. AI reflects who you are. If you are trash, the code will be trash. If you are illiterate, it is not writing you a novel.

AI IS A TYPEWRITER, NOT A CFO 4:41:05

Manual work, it does for you. Like a calculator replaced hand math. But a calculator did not become your personal financial planner, did not become your CFO. It does not assess priorities, pros, cons, risks you accept or reject. That is on you. If you are reckless, the company folds. Your fault, not the AI's. “But I followed everything the AI said.” Because you are an idiot. Do you follow everything the calculator says?

Have a GitHub. Learn to use it. Show your code. Everyone is embarrassed, “my code is ugly, they will roast me”. It is through roasting that you get feedback and improve. Praise is useless feedback. I like it when someone shows up and says “Akita, you messed this up”. I look and “you are right, let me fix this, learned something new”. Without that environment, you do not learn.
